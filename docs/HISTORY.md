# Historical Background: High Tech Gays v. DISCO

This document summarizes the real-world case that inspired **Class RAM-ifications**. It is intended for developers and players who want to understand the historical context.

---

## The Parties

### High Tech Gays (HTG)
- A social organization of gay and lesbian technology workers
- Founded in **1983** in San Jose, California (Silicon Valley)
- Members worked in defense contractors, tech firms, and government-adjacent industries
- Many required security clearances for their jobs

### Defense Industrial Security Clearance Office (DISCO)
- Federal office responsible for granting/denying security clearances to defense industry employees
- Part of the Department of Defense
- Routinely denied or restricted clearances for applicants known or suspected to be homosexual

---

## The Policy

DISCO's policy included:

1. **Automatic denial** of high-level clearances to anyone who had engaged in homosexual activity within the past 15 years
2. **Extended background investigations** for gay applicants
3. **Presumption of risk** – homosexuality was treated as a security vulnerability (e.g., blackmail risk)

This policy effectively barred many qualified LGBTQ+ workers from jobs requiring clearances, including in Silicon Valley's defense-contractor ecosystem.

---

## The Lawsuit

**1984:** High Tech Gays filed a class action lawsuit in U.S. District Court (Northern District of California) challenging DISCO's discriminatory policies.

---

## District Court (1987)

**August 1987** – Judge Thelton E. Henderson ruled in favor of the plaintiffs.

Key holdings:
- Gay people constitute a **"quasi-suspect class"** requiring heightened judicial scrutiny
- DISCO's policy failed even the **rational basis** standard
- The policy was unconstitutional

This was a significant victory – the court recognized that sexual orientation warranted stronger constitutional protection than the government had been affording.

---

## Ninth Circuit Reversal (1990)

**February 2, 1990** – The U.S. Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit reversed the District Court decision.

The appellate court:
- Applied only **rational basis** review (rejecting quasi-suspect class status)
- Stated that "homosexuality is not an immutable characteristic; it is behavioral"
- Stated that "homosexuals are not without political power"
- Upheld DISCO's policy as rationally related to national security concerns (e.g., counterintelligence targeting)

The reversal was a setback. The court's reasoning reflected outdated views about sexual orientation and ignored the practical impact of the policy on LGBTQ+ workers.

---

## Resolution (1995)

**Executive Order 12968** – President Bill Clinton issued an order prohibiting the federal government from denying security clearances based on sexual orientation.

This effectively ended the discriminatory policy and negated the impact of the Ninth Circuit's ruling. The case's legal journey was complete.
